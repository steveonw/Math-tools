Understanding the Unit Circle

The Circle: A unit circle has radius 1. Each point at angle θ gives coordinates (cos θ, sin θ). The dashed lines show projections to reveal the x-coordinate (cosine) and y-coordinate (sine) values.

The Waves: As you move counterclockwise around the circle, the y-coordinate traces out the sine wave, and the x-coordinate traces the cosine wave. One complete rotation (360° or 2π) equals one complete wave cycle. This is why sine and cosine are called periodic functions.

Learning Features:

• Reference angles - When your angle is in quadrants II, III, or IV, a faded line shows the corresponding reference angle in quadrant I. This helps you see why sin(150°) = sin(30°) but cos(150°) = -cos(30°).

• x² + y² = 1 - Watch the Pythagorean identity hold true everywhere on the circle. Since the radius is 1, the distance formula gives us cos²θ + sin²θ = 1 for any angle.

• Cofunction hints - When two angles add up to 90°, their sine and cosine values swap. Set angles 90° apart to discover this relationship: sin(30°) = cos(60°) and cos(30°) = sin(60°).

Special Angles: The golden labels around the circle mark multiples of 30°, 45°, and 60° (or π/6, π/4, and π/3 in radians). These are the angles you'll use most frequently in trigonometry because they have exact values involving √2 and √3 that you can memorize.
